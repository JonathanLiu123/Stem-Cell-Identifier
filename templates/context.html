Stem Cells and Their Potential



        Stem cells are unspecialized cells that can differentiate into various different types of cells, such as muscle cells and white blood cells. They have tremendous potential in regenerative medicine, since stem cells could theoretically be used to replace the tissues of damaged organs that cannot self-heal, such as the brain. This would allow for the development of cures for currently untreatable conditions like leukemia.

        There are 2 main types of stem cells, pluripotent and totipotent. Human adults only have pluripotent stem cells in their bodies; these stem cells are only able to differentiate into some, not all of the body's cells. For example, bone marrow stem cells can only turn into red blood cells or white blood cells, but not muscle cells. Totipotent stem cells, on the other hand, are more valuable for research and can turn into any cell in the human body, but they are only found in embryos.

 


Challenges Surrounding Stem Cells



        Both pluripotent and totipotent cells come with their risks and limitations. Due to the limits of their differentiating ability, specific pluripotent stem cells are required to regenerate particular cells, such as neurons, and these stem cells are often dangerous and difficult to extract due to their location in organs like the brain. On the other hand, extracting totipotent cells would mean stopping an embryo's normal development, which some find controversial and comparable to taking a potential life. The ethical debates surrounding these stem cells, while healthy for democracy, have led to the rise of restrictive legislation in some areas of the United States, slowing scientific progress in stem cell therapies.

Potential Solution: Induced Stem Cells



        A potential solution to these challenges is to modify the genes of normal adult cells to turn them into stem cells. These "induced" stem cells, which could be totipotent or pluripotent, would allow scientists to circumvent the challenges associated with natural stem cells. Scientists can essentially change how active a gene is, or in other words, whether a gene is on or off, to make normal cells exhibit stem cell properties. While some induced stem cells have been created in the past, many scientists continue to look for new induced stem cell lines that are more stable and differentiable, as these cells could be valuable for both research and therapeutic purposes.

        However, it is difficult to reliably predict how changing a combination of closely connected genes could impact the cell as a whole. Many genes related to cellular self-renewal, growth, and differentiation may be correlated to stem cell activity. In this case, a computational model that predicts whether a combination of genes is likely to be a stem cell based on the genetic data of known stem cells would be highly beneficial for the scientific community. Such a model would allow researchers to screen particular combinations of genes for stem cell potential before investing further time and effort into genetically modifying a cell.
